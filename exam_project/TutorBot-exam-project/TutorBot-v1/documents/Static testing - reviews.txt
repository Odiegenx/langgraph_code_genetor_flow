Static techniques test software without executing it
	They may find errors early in the life of the project
	They do not find failures, but causes of failures
	Static techniques:
	Review. Checking requirements and specification documents for errors
	before they are used in development.
	Usually manual
	Static Code Analysis. Analyse code for structural defects or 	systematic

programming weaknesses.
	• Usually automatic
	• Performed by linters and most IDEs

Reviews:

systematic examination of a document to find and remove errors.

	Objectives:
		• Finding defects
		• Gaining understanding
		• Generating discussion
		• Decision making by consensus
	Objects:
		• Requirement specifications
		• System designs
		• Code
		• Test plans
		• Test cases

Reviews and the V-model:

Every step can be reviewed.

A review is one of the earlies forms testing possible in a software development project (maybe beaten by risk analyst)

Two major types:
	Code review: can be performed one to one
	Document review Basic process:
		• The document is studied by the reviewers
		• The author is informed of the issues found
		• The author updates the document accordingly
		• A next iteration is performed, if necessary

Informal review
	• Documentation not required
	• Sometimes just a quick non-technical check
	• Inexpensive, but limited benefit
Walkthrough
	• Review meeting led by the author of the document
	• It helps team members understand the document
	• Open-ended
	• Preparation and production of a report are optional

Technical review (including code review)
	• Documented, well-defined detection process
	• Peer review, desirably with no presence of management
	• Preparation and report
Inspection (most formal)
	• Led by a trained moderator
	• Peer examination of a document
	• Pre-meeting preparation
	• Production of an inspection report
	• Formal follow-up



Formal review process:

• Planning
	• Defining the purpose, scope, and time estimation of the review
	• Selecting staff and allocating roles
	• Defining the entry and exit criteria
	• Checking that the entry criteria is met
• Initiation
	• Distributing the work product(s) to review
	• Explaining to the participants the objectives, scope, work product and roles of the review




• Individual review
	• Reviewing the work product
	• Logging potential defects, questions and comments
	• Central phase (where most errors are caught)
	• Yet optional in low-formality reviews
• Communication and analysis
	• Communicating potential defects in a meeting, log, emails, etc.
	• Analysing the defects
	• Evaluating the review results against the exit criteria
	• The work product can be fixed, or completely rejected

Review roles and responsibilities:


Manager
	• Decides to review and what to review
	• Designates staff and sets budget and timeframe
	• Not a part of the remaining review process
Author
	• Author of the work product to review
Moderator = review leader
	• Plans the review, follows up, mediates
Reviewers
Scribe = recorder
	• Documents issues


summary review report:

• Written by the moderator
• Contains
	• Date of the meeting/communication period
	• Names and roles of the participants
	• Duration
	• Size of the work product
		• E.g., pages of an SRS,
lines of code of a sw product
	• Defects found broken down by severity
		• Usually in tabular form, sorted and colour coded

Success factors for reviews:


• Objectives must be clearly predefined and agreed
• Emphasis on learning and process improvement
	• Defects are welcomed
	• Defects must be expressed objectively
	• It is the documents, not the participants, that are reviewed
• Techniques must also be reviewed
• Checklists or similar must be used

• Possible metrics to collect:
	• Time taken to review/inspect
	• Number of defects found
	• Percentage of project budget used/saved
